A damaged sewer line is not a problem you can defer. Cracked pipes, offset joints, root intrusions, and collapsed sections allow sewage to leak into surrounding soil, create sewer gas hazards inside your home, and invite further structural deterioration over time. Left unaddressed, a manageable repair becomes an emergency replacement.

How Sewer Line Repair Works

Every sewer repair starts with a camera inspection of the affected line. We identify the problem area, confirm the type of damage, and assess the overall condition of the pipe beyond the immediate issue. Based on those findings, we present you with available repair options — trenchless lining, spot repair, or full replacement — along with transparent pricing for each.

For trenchless repairs, we use CIPP (cured-in-place pipe) lining: a resin-saturated liner is inserted into the existing pipe and cured in place, creating a durable new pipe within the old one. This is minimally invasive and suited to most crack and joint failures. For severe damage or collapsed sections, traditional excavation allows us to replace the affected section directly. A final camera inspection verifies the completed repair before we leave.

Signs You Need This Service

  • Sewage backing up into basement floor drains, toilets, or bathtubs
  • Multiple fixtures draining slowly or backing up at the same time
  • Persistent sewage odors inside the home or in the yard
  • Unusually green or lush patches of grass growing above the sewer line
  • A sinkhole or ground depression forming in the yard near the sewer
  • Camera inspection already showing cracks, offsets, or root intrusion

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between trenchless repair and traditional excavation?
Trenchless methods like CIPP lining rehabilitate the existing pipe from the inside with minimal digging — usually just one or two small access points. Traditional excavation opens a trench to physically access and replace the damaged pipe section. Trenchless is faster and less disruptive to your yard; excavation is necessary for severely collapsed pipes or situations where trenchless access is not feasible.
How do I know if my sewer line needs repair or full replacement?
A camera inspection gives us the clear answer. Localized cracks, offset joints, and isolated root intrusions are typically repairable. Pipes that are extensively corroded, repeatedly collapsed, or severely deteriorated throughout are better candidates for full replacement. We show you the footage and give our honest recommendation.
How long does a sewer repair typically take?
A trenchless spot repair can often be completed in a single day. Traditional excavation repairs typically take one to three days depending on depth, soil conditions, access, and permitting requirements. We will give you a realistic timeline before the work begins and keep you updated if anything changes.
